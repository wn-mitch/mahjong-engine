## How it differs from American NMJL

The American game centers on matching a fixed pattern from the annual NMJL card. Chinese traditional is *generative*: any combination of 4 sets + 1 pair is a valid winning structure, with scoring applied after the win based on pattern-based fan (faan) rules.

Key differences that change strategy:

1. **No fixed hand list.** You aren't picking from ~70 patterns — any tile combination that forms 4 sets + 1 pair wins. This makes early-game tile reading more open-ended.
2. **Chi (runs) are legal.** Three consecutive numbers in a suit form a valid set. American doesn't allow runs except as part of specific card hands.
3. **No jokers (in most variants).** Every tile must be a natural match. No substitution flexibility.
4. **Scoring after the win.** You're not just winning — you're maximizing the post-win score via fan-counting. Some players push for high-fan hands (clean suit, all-pungs, terminals); others race for speed.
5. **No Charleston.** Round starts immediately after deal.
6. **Chi-calling restriction.** You can only call chi from the player to your left, but can call pung/kong from anyone.

## Strategic implications

- **Suit concentration**: clean-suit hands (one suit only) score heavily — early play often signals which suit a player is pursuing
- **Pung vs chi tension**: chi sets are easier to complete but score less than pung sets; the trade-off depends on table aggression
- **Defensive value of safe tiles**: with no fixed hand list, every discard is potentially someone's winning tile — late-game defense relies on reading exposures and discards more than on a known target
